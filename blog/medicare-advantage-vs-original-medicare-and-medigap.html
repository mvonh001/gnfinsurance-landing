Medicare Advantage vs. Original Medicare + Medigap: How to Think About the Choice

The biggest Medicare decision most people face — with a framework, not a verdict.

July 3·4 min read
By Michael von Heesen — Licensed Insurance Agent, Bay Harbor Benefits

This is the question almost everyone asks eventually: should I go with Medicare Advantage, or stick with Original Medicare and add a supplement? There isn't a universal right answer — the honest answer is "it depends on how you use healthcare, where you travel, and what your doctors and medications require." Here's a framework for working through it, without naming a single plan.

Two structures, not two "products"

It helps to think of this as two different structures for receiving the same underlying Medicare benefit, rather than a menu of competing products.

  • Original Medicare + Medigap: You keep Part A and Part B directly through the government program, then add a Medigap (Medicare Supplement) policy to help cover the costs Original Medicare doesn't, plus typically a standalone Part D plan for prescriptions.
  • Medicare Advantage: A private insurance company, approved by Medicare, delivers your Part A and Part B benefits (and usually Part D) through one plan, with its own network of doctors and hospitals.

Questions that actually narrow it down

Rather than starting from "which is better," these questions tend to do more useful work:

  • How often do you travel, and where? Original Medicare is accepted by any provider nationwide that takes Medicare. Medicare Advantage plans are generally network-based and more regional, though many include some out-of-network or emergency coverage.
  • Do you have specific doctors you want to keep? That only matters if those doctors are in-network for whichever Medicare Advantage plan you're considering — for Original Medicare, network isn't a factor in the same way.
  • How do you feel about cost predictability vs. structure? Medigap policies are generally built for predictable out-of-pocket costs in exchange for a monthly premium; Medicare Advantage plans often have lower monthly premiums with more variable costs tied to how much care you use.
  • Are your medications on a formulary you've checked? Whether you go the Original Medicare or Medicare Advantage route, the Part D formulary attached to your coverage is what determines how your specific medications are handled — not the structure itself.

What neither path guarantees

Neither structure is automatically cheaper, automatically more flexible, or automatically the "right" choice — despite what generic comparisons sometimes imply. A Medicare Advantage plan can be an excellent fit for someone who stays local and wants simplicity. Original Medicare with a supplement can be the better fit for someone who travels frequently or has doctors spread across networks. The honest framing is that this is a fit question, not a ranking question.

Why this can't be answered in general terms

Any article — including this one — can only give you the shape of the decision. The actual answer depends on your specific doctors, your specific medications, and how you personally use healthcare. That's exactly what a plan review is built to work through, one detail at a time.
